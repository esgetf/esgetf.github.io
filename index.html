Impact ETF 101
A quick journey to sustainability-themed exchange-traded funds (ETFs)

Impact ETFs are exchange traded funds (ETFs) which aims to create positive impact on our society and planet along with financial return. This visual journey will walk you through the basics. You may have heard the similar terms such as sustainable investing and socially-responsible investing. No matter which term we are referring is, for the impact assessment, Environment, Society, and Governance (ESG) is the most used umbrella concept by the industry and academia.

As such, ESG, literally the three core values to pursue through impact investment, is a good start for the beginners. ESG is promoted by the United Nation's Principles for Responsible Investment. It was adopted by the industry leading sustainability data providers such as MSCI, Sustainalytics, and Morningstar for the construction of their research and rating methodologies.

Based on the ESG data from the providers, the issuers of the impact ETFs may either integrate ESG as the overall theme of their portfolio or as part of their investment strategy. They screen the potential companies and exclude those who breach their ESG criterion. They may even execute their shareholder rights (e.g. voting) to alter the behaviors of the companies for sustainable pursuit.

A deeper dive: What are ESG & ETF?
What is ETF?

An exchange traded fund (ETF) is a type of investment fund holding a set of securities, such as stocks and bonds. Most ETFs are passive ETFs, which replicate the holdings of an established index. It means they hold the same securities in the same proportions as a certain stock market index or bond market index, for example, the S&P 500 Index, the total market index, and the NASDAQ-100 index.

An ETF divides ownership of itself into shares, which you can buy and sell at stock exchanges like what you do with your stocks. In other words, the shareholders of an ETF indirectly own the assets of the fund.

Vanguard S&P 500 ETF (VOO) and its holdings
VOO tracks the S&P 500, which is a market-cap-weighted index of 505 large-cap U.S. stocks. It represents approximately 80% of the market value of the U.S. stock market. Often synonymous with "the market" in the U.S., the S&P 500 is the closest thing to a default U.S. stock index.

What is ESG?

Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) refers to the three core factors in measuring the sustainability and societal impact of an investment in a company or business. You may have been familiar with some of the typical ESG issues, such as climate change, pollution, modern slavery, child labour, employee relations, corruption, executive pay, and tax transparency.

The development of ESG can be traced back to a famous debate about the purpose of a corporation in the late 20th century. Should a corporation set the maximization of its profit for its shareholders as its ultimate and only goal? Or should a corporation consider and align the interests of all its stakeholders, not just shareholders? The two extremes represent the shareholder theory and the stakeholder theory respectively. The modern corporations and business organizations such as World Economic Forum and the U.S. Business Roundtable have left the shareholder theory doctrine in favour of the stakeholder theory, so did some prestigious business and management thinkers.

The concept of stakeholders is the great context where ESG lays. ESG has been used to build up metrics for investors to evaluate the impact of a corporation it brings to its stakeholders. Shareholder’s engagement through ESG can be a heal between the two camps - corporations should bring not only financial return to their shareholders but also benefits to wilder social members of their communities.

A breakdown of the key issues under ESG
There are quite a few different metrics under the ESG themes. Let's take the methodology of MSCI ESG ratings as an example. MSCI is one of the leading ESG data providers around the globe. Some of the largest & most prestigious ESG ETFs have been using the ratings from MSCI since their inception, for instance, iShares MSCI KLD 400 Social ETF (DSI), Vanguard ESG US Stock ETF (ESGV), Xtrackers MSCI USA ESG Leaders Equity ETF (USSG), and iShares ESG MSCI USA Leaders ETF (SUSL).
Trend
ESG-themed ETFs: capitalizing on a socially responsible future
Fact

ESG-mandated assets in the United States could grow almost three times as fast as non-ESG-mandated assets to comprise half of all professionally managed investments by 2025.

As emerging technologies, such as AI, enable better-quality ESG data and the regulatory landscape becomes clearer, institutional, and retail investors are expected to increasingly demand that ESG factors be applied to a greater percentage of their portfolios. In this scenario, ESG assets should continue to grow at a 16 percent compound annual growth rate (CAGR), totaling almost US$35 trillion by 2025 (figure 1).

The numbers of ESG funds soars on a global scale

According to the IMF, from 2010 to 2019, the number of ESG-dedicated funds (including ETFs) has risen from 913 to 1931, with assets growing from $352 billion to $856 billion.

The returns of all the ten top ESG ETFs outperforms full market

Close to six out of 10 sustainable funds delivered higher returns than equivalent conventional funds over the past decade, according to a study that undermines claims that investing based on environmental, social and governance principles hampers performance.

How do the ESG ETFs make impact?

In September 2020, justETF, therefore, surveyed the 15 largest ETF providers who together manage around €600 billion in equity ETFs. The results show that, contrary to frequent criticism, ETF providers are handling the voting rights from ETF holdings in a responsible manner. Only one small provider stated that it did not exercise voting rights at all. 12 providers, who managed €530 billion in their equity ETFs at the time of the survey, exercise voting rights across the entire range of equity ETFs - with no exceptions. The exercise of rights is not limited to sustainable ETFs.
